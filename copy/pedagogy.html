Ivanhoe and Pedagogy

Ivanhoe has hitherto been theorized and used as a pedagogical aid. In 2004, Jerome McGann promoted Ivanhoe as a way to “to promote rigorous as well as imaginative thinking; to develop habits of thoroughness and flexibility when we investigate our cultural inheritance and try to exploit its sources and resources; and to expose and promote the collaborative dynamics of all humane studies.” The role-play aspect of the Ivanhoe experience is critical to meet these objectives, for role play requires students to reinterpret a cultural text through collaborative performance and reflection. By self-consciously embodying an element in the text, students must internalize and synthesize (rather than summarize) aspects of the story, apply relevant theory or historical context, and evaluate their moves in relationship to those of other players. While the use of roles and moves facilitates the performance of a student's role, Ivanhoe’s unique Role Journal feature encourages students to reflect upon their moves, motivations, and interpretations.

There are a number of pedagogical guides that embrace not only play, but specifically role-play as way to engage and motivate students in diverse fields and settings. Below, we have included some recent publications that explore the theoretical underpinnings of educational play, as well as examples of successful role-play assignments.

Bibliography

Ivanhoe:

  • “03/14/2002: ‘A Bump of Reverence'.” Speculative Computing (15 Mar. 2002): vii-xviii.

    Archived excerpt from the original Ivanhoe game.

  • “Applied Research in Patacriticism.” Wikipedia (15 Mar. 2002): vii-xviii.

    Overview of Jerome McGann’s DH initiative that led to Ivanhoe, as well as to NINES, Juxta, and other digital tools.

  • Drucker, Johanna. SpecLab: Digital Aesthetics and Projects in Speculative Computing. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2009.

    A general history of Drucker’s SpecLab DH initiative, devoted to pursuing “risky projects with serious aims.”

  • Drucker, Johanna, and Geoffrey Rockwell. “Introduction: Reflections on the Ivanhoe Game.” TEXT Technology 2 (2003): vii-xviii.

    History of Ivanhoe, and overview as of 2003, as well as an introduction via the “Turn of the Screw” game.

  • McGann, Jerome, with Johanna Drucker and Bethany Nowviskie. “IVANHOE: Education in a New Key,” Romantic Circles (Dec. 2014)

    Overview of Ivanhoe as of late 2004, with speculation on its application to pedagogy in particular.

  • Nowviskie, Bethany. "Sketching Ivanhoe.” nowviskie.org (5 Jun. 2009).

    Reflection and retrospection on Ivanhoe, with images taken from the increasingly complex design process.

  • Sansing, Chandler. “Case Study and Appeal: Building the Ivanhoe Game for Classroom Flexibility.” TEXT Technology 2 (2003): 43-52.

    A use of Ivanhoe within a middle-school classroom, with speculation as to its further adaptability.

  • Willett, Rebekah, Muriel Robinson, and Jackie March, eds. Play, Creativity and Digital Cultures. New York: Routledge, 2009.
